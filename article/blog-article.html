How a single automation idea evolved from a rough prototype into a structured intake flow — and what each version revealed about the problem.

The starting point

Most client intake processes are a series of manual handoffs disguised as a system. A form gets submitted, someone reads it, a follow-up email gets written, a calendar link gets sent. Each step is a potential delay, a point of inconsistency, a place where context gets quietly lost.

This build started from a real friction point: onboarding new enquiries was eating into time that should have been spent doing the work itself. The goal wasn't simply to automate — it was to think clearly about what a well-designed intake actually looks like, then build toward it.

Automation doesn't fix a broken process. It accelerates it.

Starting constraint, before v1

What the system had to do

Before writing a single line of logic, I mapped the intake journey from the client's perspective and from ours. The system needed to qualify the enquiry, route it correctly, acknowledge the client immediately, and surface the right information to the right person — without any manual reading in between.

Every automation node should earn its place. If a step doesn't transform, filter, or route the information — remove it.

Version Progression
Version 01 Jan 2026 Foundation

The working skeleton

Version 1 was about proving the concept worked end-to-end. A Typeform intake feeds into a Make.com scenario that logs the submission, sends an auto-acknowledgement, and creates a Notion card. Nothing clever — just the minimum viable loop, running reliably.

  • Typeform → Make.com → Notion pipeline established
  • Auto-acknowledgement email triggered on submission
  • All enquiries treated equally — no qualification logic yet
  • Manual review still required before any response

Version 02 Feb 2026 Refinement

Adding intelligence

The v1 skeleton revealed the real gap: every enquiry looked the same to the system, even when they weren't. V2 introduced an AI classification layer — a prompt that reads the submission and assigns a priority tier, a service category, and a suggested response tone. The Notion card now arrives with context, not just data.

  • Claude API classification step added mid-pipeline
  • Three priority tiers: Immediate / Standard / Exploratory
  • Service category auto-tagged from enquiry content
  • Acknowledgement email personalised by tier
  • Notion card enriched with AI summary + suggested next step

Version 03 Mar 2026 Current

Closing the loop

V3 moved from intake to conversation. The system now generates a first-draft response for the studio to review and send — informed by the classification, the client's stated goal, and the relevant service layer. The human decision becomes an edit, not a composition. Review time dropped from ~18 minutes to under 3.

  • Draft response generated and attached to Notion card
  • Response tone matched to enquiry tier and service category
  • Studio reviews and approves via a single Notion button
  • Sent email logged back into the Notion card automatically
  • Full audit trail: submission → classification → draft → sent

What this build actually taught

The version progression matters more than the final state. V1 proved the pipeline. V2 proved the model. V3 proved the interface. Each version asked a different question — and each answer changed what the next version needed to be.

The lesson isn't about automation. It's about knowing which question you're answering at each stage, and not trying to answer all of them at once.
